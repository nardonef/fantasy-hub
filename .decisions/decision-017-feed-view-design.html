Feed View Design

Visual

The Feed is the root view of your new Intel tab -- it's where users land when they tap "Intel" in the tab bar. It needs to blend content from multiple sources (Reddit posts, Bluesky mentions, rankings changes, stat lines) into a cohesive, filterable stream. The design challenge: each source type has a different shape (a Reddit post with upvotes vs. a ranking change vs. a stat line), but they need to feel unified. You also need filtering -- by team, by player, by source -- without overwhelming the screen with controls.

A single scrollable list of cards, each with a colored left border indicating the source (Reddit=orange, Bluesky=blue, Rankings=green, Stats=navy). All cards share the same basic structure: source icon + timestamp at top, content in the middle, player name + metadata at bottom. Filter chips at the top let you switch between "My Team", "All Players", "Waivers", or tap a specific player. This is the most straightforward design -- it looks like a social feed you're already used to, but every card is relevant to fantasy. I recommend this because it's familiar, scannable, and the source colors make it easy to see the mix at a glance.

Works well when

  • Familiar social feed pattern -- no learning curve
  • Source color coding makes the feed scannable at a glance
  • Simple filter chips handle team/player/source filtering

Watch out for

  • All cards same size -- ranking changes don't need as much space as Reddit posts
  • Long Reddit excerpts could dominate the feed visually

Instead of mixing all sources into one stream, group them into visual "lanes" -- Reddit Buzz at the top, Ranking Moves in the middle (as a compact horizontal scroll), Expert Takes (Bluesky) below, and Stats at the bottom. Each section has its own visual treatment optimized for its data shape. Filter tabs at the top let you isolate one source. This gives each data type the layout it deserves -- rankings look like a leaderboard, social posts look like a feed, stats look like a table.

Works well when

  • Each source type gets a layout optimized for its data shape
  • Rankings as compact cards are more scannable than full-width cards
  • Users who care about one source type can find it instantly

Watch out for

  • Sections can feel disconnected -- you lose the "everything about player X" view
  • More complex to build -- each section needs its own layout

Group all signals by player, not by time or source. Each player with recent activity gets a card showing all their signals in a compact stack -- the latest Reddit mention, current ranking, recent stat line, all in one place. Small colored dots indicate which sources have data. Tapping the card opens the full Player Detail view. This is the most "intelligence dashboard" approach -- you see everything about each player at once instead of scrolling through a mixed timeline.

Works well when

  • Users care about players, not sources -- everything about a player in one place
  • Natural entry point to Player Detail view (tap to expand)
  • Signal density indicators help prioritize which players to investigate

Watch out for

  • Loses the "what's new" chronological feel of a traditional feed
  • Players with many signals get long cards; single-signal players feel sparse

A horizontal "trending players" ticker at the top (think ESPN bottom line, but for your roster) shows which players have the most signal activity. Below it, a chronological card stream shows the actual content. Tapping a player in the ticker filters the stream to that player. This gives you both a "what's hot" overview and a deep-dive feed in one screen. The broadcast ticker ties into your "Broadcast Dark" visual identity.

Works well when

  • Ticker gives instant "what's hot" visibility without scrolling
  • Broadcast aesthetic aligns perfectly with your design system
  • Ticker-to-filter is a natural interaction pattern

Watch out for

  • Ticker takes vertical space on an already dense screen
  • Two scrollable regions (horizontal ticker + vertical feed) can feel fiddly
