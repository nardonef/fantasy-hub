Analytics Dashboard UX

Interaction

The Analytics tab is the heart of Fantasy League Hub — it's where users explore the insights no single platform provides. The question is how to organize a deep analytics surface on a phone screen. You've got head-to-head records, luck indexes, scoring trends, consistency ratings, draft grades, power rankings, playoff performance, and champion histories — potentially dozens of views. How do users navigate all of this without it feeling like a spreadsheet? And how do you make the data feel alive and storytelling-driven rather than just rows of numbers?

One long vertical scroll divided into clearly labeled sections: Standings & Rankings, Head-to-Head, Scoring & Trends, Draft Analysis, Records & Milestones. Each section shows a compact preview (top 3 in a ranking, a mini chart, a headline stat) with a "See All" link that pushes to a full detail view. A sticky season filter at the top lets you toggle between current season, all-time, or a custom range — and every section updates. This is the Apple Health / Fitness model: scroll to browse, tap to dive deep. I recommend this because it's the most discoverable — users see everything available just by scrolling, and the season filter gives them control over the time dimension without navigating away.

Works well when

  • Most discoverable — scroll to see every analytics category
  • Season filter controls time dimension globally
  • Compact previews + "See All" keeps the scroll manageable

Watch out for

  • Long scroll with many sections can feel overwhelming
  • Users who want one specific stat have to scroll to find it

Organize analytics around people, not categories. The Analytics tab opens to a grid of all league managers (avatars/initials). Tap any manager to see their full profile: career record, scoring trends, draft history, luck index, playoff performance, and head-to-head records against everyone. A "Compare" button lets you pick two managers for a side-by-side breakdown. League-wide stats (records, champion history) live in a "League Records" section below the manager grid. This approach leans into the social/rivalry aspect — fantasy is about people, not abstract metrics. Browsing is driven by curiosity about specific managers.

Works well when

  • Leans into rivalries — "let me check Mike's stats"
  • Manager profiles are highly shareable (screenshots)
  • Natural entry point — people are more interesting than categories

Watch out for

  • League-wide trends (scoring over time, draft patterns) are less prominent
  • Users need to know who they want to look at
  • Comparing more than 2 managers is cumbersome

AI-Curated Insight Cards

1 "Frank has a 7-1 record vs. Mike since 2020"
↓
2 "League scoring is up 12% vs last season" + chart
↓
3 "Draft grade rankings: Frank A+, Mike C-"
↓
4 "Luck Index: you've been unlucky 4 of 5 weeks"
↓
5 Tap any card → full detail view
Instead of organizing by category, let the AI curate what's most interesting right now. The Analytics tab shows a feed of insight cards — each one is a bite-sized stat with a headline, a mini visualization, and context. The AI picks what to surface based on recency (what changed this week), relevance (your team, your rivals), and interestingness (unusual patterns, records being approached). Tap any card to drill into the full analysis. New cards appear after each week's games. This makes analytics feel alive and editorial rather than static tables — like a personalized stats newsletter.

Works well when

  • Most engaging — always something new and relevant
  • AI curation surfaces insights users wouldn't find on their own
  • Feels alive — new cards after each week

Watch out for

  • Hard to find a specific stat on demand
  • AI curation quality is make-or-break — bad picks kill trust
  • Ongoing LLM costs for generating insight cards

Each tab is a full-screen analytics view
A horizontal swipeable tab bar at the top of the Analytics screen: Rankings, H2H, Trends, Draft, Records. Each tab is its own full-screen analytics view — swipe left/right to move between them. This is the ESPN/Yahoo pattern (think their "Scoreboard" / "Standings" / "Players" tabs). Each sub-tab gets the full screen, so you can show rich charts, detailed tables, and interactive elements without space constraints. The trade-off is that you can't see what's in other tabs without swiping — less discoverable than a scroll, but each view is more focused.

Works well when

  • Each category gets full screen — room for rich charts/tables
  • Familiar pattern from ESPN, Yahoo, and most sports apps
  • Swipe between sections is fast and fluid

Watch out for

  • Less discoverable — can't see everything at a glance
  • 5+ tabs feels crowded in horizontal space
  • Users may not realize there are more tabs off-screen
