Navigation & App Structure

Information Architecture

Fantasy League Hub has a lot of surface area: multiple leagues, years of history per league, deep analytics, an AI chat, manager profiles, and social features. The navigation needs to make all of this discoverable without being overwhelming. On iOS, the standard pattern is a tab bar at the bottom — but how many tabs, what goes in each one, and how league-switching works are all design decisions. A user in three leagues across Yahoo and ESPN needs to move between them fluidly without losing context.

A 5-tab bottom bar (Dashboard, Analytics, AI Chat, League, Profile) with a league switcher in the top navigation bar. The league switcher is always visible — tap it to switch between your connected leagues, and the entire app context changes. The Dashboard tab is your home: this week's matchup, power rankings, recent activity. Analytics is the deep dive: head-to-head, trends, records. League shows members, standings, transactions. This is the standard iOS pattern and the one users already know. I recommend it because every tab has a clear purpose, the league switcher keeps context-switching fast, and it scales well as you add features (they nest under existing tabs).

Works well when

  • Familiar iOS pattern — users know how tab bars work
  • League switcher keeps multi-league navigation fast
  • Each tab has clear ownership — easy to add features

Watch out for

  • 5 tabs is the max before it feels crowded
  • Analytics and Dashboard might overlap in users' mental model

One central feed that mixes content from all your leagues — like a fantasy football news feed. Cards show this week's matchups across all leagues, insights ("You're on a 4-game streak in Dynasty League"), AI-generated storylines, trade alerts, and waiver results. Tap any card to drill into that specific league's full view. No tab bar — navigation is driven by the content itself. This is the Sleeper/Twitter model: scroll through a feed, tap to go deeper. Great for engagement and cross-league discovery, but it can be harder to find specific data when you want it.

Works well when

  • Best for engagement — always something to scroll through
  • Cross-league insights surface naturally
  • AI suggestions and narratives feel native in a feed

Watch out for

  • Harder to find specific stats — no clear "go here for X"
  • Feed curation is complex to build well
  • Can feel chaotic with many leagues

Think Discord or Slack — your first screen is a list of your leagues, like "rooms." Tap into one and you're fully inside that league's world: its own dashboard, analytics, chat, history, and members. The league room metaphor is strong because each league is its own community with its own context. Swipe back to return to the league list. An "All Leagues" room at the top aggregates cross-league stats. This works well for the social angle — each league feels like its own space. The downside is more taps to get anywhere (open app → pick league → find feature) compared to a tab bar.

Works well when

  • Each league feels like its own distinct space
  • Natural for the social/community aspect
  • Clean separation — no context confusion between leagues

Watch out for

  • More taps to reach content — league list → league → feature
  • Harder to compare across leagues
  • Users with one league get unnecessary indirection

Just three tabs: Home, AI, and You. Home is a vertically scrollable page that contains everything for the current league — this week's matchup at the top, then power rankings, then analytics highlights, then recent transactions. It's one long page you scroll through rather than navigating between tabs. AI is the dedicated chat assistant. You is your profile, settings, and cross-league career stats. The league switcher in the top bar changes which league Home shows. This is the simplest structure — fewer decisions about "where does this feature go?" because the answer is always "on the Home scroll."

Works well when

  • Simplest to build and maintain — everything on one scroll
  • AI chat gets its own dedicated tab (prominent placement)
  • No navigation debates — new features go on Home

Watch out for

  • Home becomes a very long scroll — hard to find specific things
  • Analytics don't get the dedicated space they deserve
  • Scales poorly — Home becomes a junk drawer as features grow
