Recommendations Card Design

Visual

Your feed will mix social posts, ranking changes, and recommendation signals — but not all signals are equal. A consensus top waiver pickup backed by 3 sources is very different from a speculative Reddit hot take. This decision is about how a recommendation card communicates what it knows, how confident it is, and what the user should do about it. The card needs to fit cleanly in the unified stream alongside other card types while still giving users enough signal to act.

Every card leads with an urgency label — red "Act Now", yellow "Watch", or grey "FYI" — determined by how time-sensitive the underlying signal is (injury news = Act Now, OTA report = FYI). The border color mirrors the urgency. Users who scan the feed quickly can filter by color without reading the content. Good for a pre-game window when urgency is everything, but the labels can feel alarmist for routine signals.

Works well when

  • Users check the feed close to game time when urgency matters most
  • Color coding lets you scan 10 cards in 3 seconds
  • Injury news needs to be surfaced immediately above everything else

Watch out for

  • "Act Now" labels go stale — a Wednesday injury alert is not urgent on Sunday
  • Everything feels urgent when there's a lot of news — label inflation

Each card displays a confidence score — 3 green dots (strong consensus across multiple sources) down to 1 yellow dot (single-source speculation). The score is computed from how many independent sources agree and how authoritative they are (FantasyPros consensus = more weight than a single Reddit post). This is the most honest design: it directly communicates the difference between "everyone agrees" and "one guy on Reddit thinks." I recommend this because it lets users make their own risk calls rather than the app pretending it knows what's urgent.

Works well when

  • Signal quality varies wildly — 3-source consensus vs single hot take need different treatment
  • Users want to make their own calls, not just follow instructions
  • Source diversity grows over time — the score naturally improves as you add sources

Watch out for

  • Confidence scoring logic needs to be tuned — bad weights produce misleading scores
  • Dot indicator may not be intuitive to all users on first look

Every recommendation card has an explicit action button — Start, Sit, Add, or Drop — positioned on the right edge of the card. The button color maps to the action type (green for positive, red for drop). This is the most opinionated design: the app decides what you should do and puts a button on it. It creates a direct path from "I see the recommendation" to "I took action." The tradeoff is that the app is making a judgment call the user might not agree with.

Works well when

  • Users want to act quickly — one tap from recommendation to decision
  • The recommendation logic is solid enough to earn user trust
  • Clear action hierarchy (Start > Add > Sit > Drop) is well defined

Watch out for

  • Buttons imply the app has roster access — requires provider OAuth integration
  • Wrong recommendations feel much worse when the UI says "do this"

Recommendation rows blend with ranking change rows
Recommendation cards use the same compact single-row format as ranking change cards and social snippets — just a colored action label, player name, and source. No extra chrome, no buttons, no urgency theatrics. This design prioritizes stream density over individual card expressiveness. You see more signals per scroll, and the feed feels cohesive rather than having recommendation cards stand out as a different UI element. The tradeoff is that each card communicates less — there's no room for context or confidence signals.

Works well when

  • Feed density matters — pack more signals into the same screen height
  • You want a visually cohesive stream where all cards feel like siblings
  • Users are familiar enough with fantasy to need just a name + action

Watch out for

  • No confidence or context visible — every signal looks equally credible
  • Thin cards feel low-value compared to richer social post cards in the same feed
