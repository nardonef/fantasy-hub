AI Chat Experience

Interaction

You've already decided on RAG + Tool Use (Decision 6) for the AI backend. This decision is about the front-end experience — what the chat feels like to use. How does a user start a conversation? What does the AI respond with — just text, or rich cards with charts and data? How does it handle follow-ups? And critically: does the AI feel like a separate "chat feature" bolted on, or is it woven into the whole app — accessible from analytics screens, player pages, and the dashboard? The chat UI defines whether this feels like a gimmick or a genuine league intelligence tool.

Full-Screen Chat + Contextual Triggers

The AI Chat tab (the third tab in the nav bar) opens to a full-screen conversational interface. The chat shows rich responses — not just text, but inline stat cards, mini charts, and data tables rendered natively in SwiftUI. After each response, the AI suggests 2-3 follow-up questions as tappable pills. The key differentiator: contextual triggers throughout the app. On any analytics screen, a small "Ask AI" button lets you start a chat pre-loaded with that context. Viewing head-to-head records? Tap "Ask AI" and the chat opens with "Tell me about the Frank vs Mike rivalry" already seeded. I recommend this because it gives the AI a dedicated home for deep conversations while making it accessible everywhere — best of both worlds.

Works well when

  • Full screen means room for rich responses with charts and tables
  • Contextual "Ask AI" buttons make the chat feel integrated, not siloed
  • Follow-up pills guide users who don't know what to ask

Watch out for

  • Switching to the chat tab loses your place in analytics
  • Users might not discover the "Ask AI" triggers on other screens

Slide-Up Assistant Sheet

1 Tap floating button on any screen
↓
2 Half-sheet slides up over current view
↓
3 AI already knows what screen you're on
↓
4 Ask a question — response appears in sheet
↓
5 Drag up for full-screen, swipe down to dismiss
No dedicated chat tab at all. Instead, a floating action button (FAB) appears on every screen. Tap it and a half-sheet slides up from the bottom — the AI is instantly available without leaving your current context. The AI automatically knows what you're looking at (which analytics screen, which manager profile, which matchup). You can drag the sheet to full-screen for longer conversations or swipe it down to dismiss. This is the Apple Maps / Shazam pattern — the assistant is a layer on top of the app, not a separate destination. The trade-off: less room for rich responses in the half-sheet view, and the FAB takes up screen real estate.

Works well when

  • Zero context switching — ask without leaving your screen
  • AI auto-knows what you're looking at
  • Feels like a native iOS assistant, not a chat app

Watch out for

  • Half-sheet has limited space for rich responses
  • FAB can obscure content on smaller screens
  • Long conversations get awkward in an overlay

Spotlight Command Bar

1 Pull down from top of any screen
↓
2 Search bar appears with recent queries
↓
3 Type natural language or search terms
↓
4 Inline result card with answer + data
↓
5 Tap to navigate to relevant analytics view
Instead of a chat interface, use a Spotlight-style command bar. Pull down from the top of any screen (like iOS Spotlight) and type anything — natural language queries, player names, stat lookups. Results appear as compact answer cards: "Mike's all-time record: 87-63" with a link to the full H2H view. It's more search engine than chatbot — optimized for quick lookups rather than conversations. There's no conversation history; each query is standalone. This is the fastest path to a specific answer, but it doesn't support the back-and-forth that makes AI feel intelligent. Think of it as a league search engine powered by AI, not a conversational assistant.

Works well when

  • Fastest path from question to answer — no chat overhead
  • Familiar iOS Spotlight gesture
  • Doubles as universal search for the app

Watch out for

  • No conversation — can't do follow-ups like "now compare that to 2022"
  • Complex questions need multiple lookups
  • Misses the narrative/storytelling potential of conversational AI

Proactive Narrative Feed

THIS WEEK'S STORYLINES
RIVALRY WATCH
Frank vs Mike — Week 8 rematch. Mike has won 3 straight, but Frank's roster is peaking at the right time. Here's why this week is different...
DRAFT REPORT CARD
Mid-season draft grades are in. Sarah's early-round strategy is paying dividends — her picks are outscoring ADP by 23%.
Tap any story to chat about it
The AI tab isn't a blank chat — it's a curated narrative feed. Each week, the AI writes storylines: rivalry previews, power ranking commentary, draft grade updates, milestone alerts, and season recaps. These read like a fantasy column written by someone who knows your league intimately. Tap any narrative to enter a conversational thread about that topic. You can also type a fresh question at the bottom. This flips the dynamic: instead of the user always initiating, the AI proactively tells you what's interesting. The risk is that if the narratives aren't compelling, the whole tab feels like AI-generated filler.

Works well when

  • AI initiates — users don't need to know what to ask
  • Weekly storylines create appointment engagement
  • Narratives are highly shareable in league group chats

Watch out for

  • Higher LLM cost — generating narratives proactively
  • Quality bar is very high — bad narratives feel like spam
  • Specific stat lookups are buried under the feed
